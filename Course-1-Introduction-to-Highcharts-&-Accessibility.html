Module 1: The Highcharts Accessibility Module

Lesson 1: What is the Highcharts Accessibility module?

Why an Accessibility module?

Creating accessible data visualizations is both a technical challenge and a moral imperative. Charts and graphs are central to how people interpret data, but for many users — including those relying on screen readers, voice input, or other assistive technologies — standard web charts can present serious barriers to understanding.

The Highcharts Accessibility Module helps developers overcome those challenges. It extends the functionality of Highcharts to make charts perceivable, operable, and understandable to all users, regardless of their abilities or the tools they use.

By including the Accessibility Module in your chart setup, you can:

  • Enable screen reader and keyboard navigation support.
  • Make charts accessibile for users with low vision.
  • Ensure your visualizations meet the WC3's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG.)

Beyond meeting compliance requirements, accessible charts make your data more inclusive and usable for everyone , improving the experience for all audiences, not just those with disabilities.

Voice Input

The Highcharts Accessibility Module supports voice input software, allowing users to interact with chart controls using voice commands.

Here’s a brief demo video of how it works,

JS Fiddle
Codepen

As you can see in the demo, voice input works like keyboard navigation, except you’re obviously using your voice instead of typing.

To enable voice input support, just include the accessibility module in your HTML file, and users can use compatible voice recognition software, such as Nuance Dragon or Microsoft Windows Speech Recognition, to navigate and control chart features.

Cognitive Accessibility

A lot of Highcharts features have been developed with the goal of making it charts easier to use and understand. This translates to a better experience for everyone, but especially for people with cognitive disabilities.

What is a cognitive disability? It could be challenges with memory, issues with maintaining focus, difficulty reading or comprehending information, or abstract thinking and analysis – in other words, a lot of the mental tasks users undertake when analyzing data visualizations.

In order to alleviate some of these challenges, Highcharts comes with numerous built-in features that are on display in the demo below.

For example, chart legends and series labels provide a lot of persistent information about the data overall, making it easier to identify the series via consistently used text, color and patterns.

When you roll over one of the lines, you’ll see that the other series dim, and and a tooltip appears that gives me information about the specific point, making focusing on individual data points much more manageable for users, regardless of their cognitive abilities.

Another helpful feature supporting focus is the ability to view the data as a data table.

Click on the context menu icon in the top right corner of the chart and click on the "View data table" option.

Referring to a straightforward data table alongside the data visualization may make the chart easier to understand for people who find the chart alone confusing.

ANother option in the context menu drop down is "View in full screen," and this is a great option for users who need extra support focusing as it removes all surrounding distractions, like the browser chrome and other apps.

Tactile Export

Highcharts also supports export formats that can be used with embossing printers.

Embossing printers, also called Braille embossers, are devices that create a raised-dot, tactile version of digital content, most commonly for blind and visually impaired individuals. They take computer data and emboss it onto heavyweight paper to produce Braille text and tactile graphics like charts or maps, making information accessible through touch.

If you export your Highcharts chart as an SVG vector image, you can print it on an embosser and create a tactile version of your chart, making the data accessible through touch.

Setting playback duration

As we saw when you made our first audio chart, many basic audio configuration options are handled for you out of the box, but there are basic tweaks we can make to our configuration that will enhance the chart’s audio experience and give the user more insight into your data. One of these methods is configuring the duration of the audio.

Here’s a basic demo where we have some sonified points that play in order of their y values, the lowest y values playing first. The playback duration set to "slow" (4 seconds.) Press the "Play Chart" button, and as it plays, you get a pretty good sense of the range of the data. And there’s enough time between notes to comprehend differences between each point.

JS Fiddle
Codepen

Now change the playback speed to "fast" using the radio buttons below the chart. Now the chart plays very quickly, and we only get a sense of the lowest and highest notes and not much in between.

But one benefit of fast playback is that we're able to more easily hear an overall trend in the data in terms of its high and low points.

So by changing the duration of the audio, we changed the focus of the data. At the slower the duration, its easier for the listener to comprehend individual points. At the faster the duration, the sounds communicate more of an overall trend in the data.

Configuring playback order

Another configuration option that can enhance the audio presentation of your data is the sonification configuration option called “order.”

Here’s the chart we sonified in a previous lesson, and when you click "Play chart", each series plays one after the other, or sequentially, and this is the default setting for the sonification order setting. Ordering your playback sequentially is a great way to enable the user to focus on one series at a time.

JS Fiddle
Codepen
But if you look at the series, notice that the purple series ends at 3 on the x axis. Playing the series sequentially, I may not pick up on that fact because I’m focused on each individual series rather than the chart as a whole.

Use the radio buttons below the chart to change the play order setting to simultaneous, and play the chart again. Notice that it's much more obvious that the second series drops off about halfway through the chart playback as compared to the first, and this intriguing detail makes me want to investigate the data more to find out why that happens. And this is one example of how simultaneous playback can surface insights in your data.

Conditional audio tracks

One of the most effective ways to enhance the audio presentation of your data is to use different sounds at different times for different reasons.

For example, in this demo that shows monthly average temperatures in Helsinki, there's a visual demarcation for the summer months, mid April through mid September, represented by the green plot area labeled Summer.

JS Fiddle
Codepen

Play the chart, and you'll notice that when the playback enters those summer months, you hear a soft shaker sound indicating that playback has entered the green plot area.

Also notice the plot line at zero degrees celsius and the constant tone that plays to give the user context about how far above or below each data point is from zero.

All of this sound layering is accomplished through track configuration.

There are two types of tracks, and both can be configured globally for the entire chart or at the series level, and they can either be configured as instruments or speech.

The first type of tracks are just called “tracks” and these are used for sonifying your series data.

The second type of tracks are called context tracks. These tracks are not dependent on series data and can be configured to play when certain conditions are met to provide context as the chart plays, such as the constant tone indicating zero and the soft shaker sound of the summer months in the demo above.
