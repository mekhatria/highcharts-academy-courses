Course Scope

This course focuses on how to create effective data visualizations using Highcharts Chart Chooser and a data visualization framework.

What you will learn

  • Apply a data visualization framework based on context, audience, and data type to create compelling charts.
  • Apply basic design principles for data visualization.
  • Use Highcharts Chart Chooser to select appropriate chart types.

Not covered

  • Data cleaning.
  • Advanced Highcharts coding.
  • Statistical analysis.

Prerequisites

Required

  • Basic knowledge of JavaScript, HTML, and CSS.
  • Familiarity with common chart types (bar, line, pie).
  • Understanding of basic business metrics.

Recommended

  • Experience working with data in spreadsheets.

Not required

  • Advanced programming.
  • Prior Highcharts experience.
  • Statistics background.

Lesson 2: Context - What problem are you solving?

What you will learn

  • How to identify your context (strategic, operational, or analytical)
  • Why context determines what chart to use.

Context is the "why" behind your chart. Why are you creating this visualization? What decision needs to be made?

Most people skip this question. They go straight from "I have data" to "I'll make a chart." But if you don't know what problem you're solving, you can't know if your chart is effective.

Examples of context

1. Strategic Context

  • Big decisions with long-term impact
  • Usually annual or quarterly
  • Executive audience
  • Example: Which product should we invest in next year?

2. Operational Context

  • Day-to-day decisions
  • Daily or weekly checks
  • Operations teams
  • Example: Are we on track to hit this month's targets?

3. Analytical Context

  • Exploring and investigating
  • Looking for patterns
  • Data analysts
  • Example: What patterns exist in our sales data?

How to identify your context

  • Is it a big-picture, long-term call?

    Then you are working in a strategic context, so keep things simple, straightforward, and focused on the decision itself.

  • Are you checking whether things are on track day-to-day?

    That is an operational context; in this case, focus on highlighting trends, targets, and recent data.

  • Are you digging into patterns or exploring the data for insights?

    Then it is an analytical context, so go deeper with detail, comparisons, and multiple dimensions.

Lesson 3: Audience - Who needs this?

What you will learn

  • How to match the level of complexity to your audience's needs.

You can have the perfect chart for your context, but if your audience can't understand it, you've failed.

Different people need different things. A chart that works for a technical team might confuse an executive. A chart that works for an executive might frustrate an analyst who needs details.

Examples of audience

1. Executives

  • Very busy
  • Need to make decisions (What should I do?)
  • Want the big picture (not details)

2. Technical Teams

  • Want details (How does this work?)
  • Care about accuracy (Show me the data)
  • Comfortable with complexity (Can handle multiple things at once)

3. General Audiences

  • Mixed knowledge (some experts, some beginners)
  • Different goals (some decide, some learn, some just check)

Lesson 4: Data - What type do you have?

What you will learn

  • How to distinguish categorical from continuous data.
  • Which charts work with categorical vs. continuous data.

Context tells you what to show. Audience tells you how to show it. But data tells you what's even possible.

Not all charts work with all data. You can't make a line chart from categories. You can't make a pie chart from time series data. Understanding your data type is fundamental.

Two Data Types

1. Categorical Data

Examples:

  • Products: Product A, Product B, Product C
  • Regions: North, South, East, West
  • Departments: Sales, Marketing, Engineering

Examples of suitable charts: Column, Bar, Pie

2. Continuous Data

Examples:

  • Time: January, February, March...
  • Temperature: 15°C, 20°C, 25°C
  • Money: $45,230, $52,180...

Examples of suitable charts: Line, Area, Scatter

Lesson 2: How Chart Chooser Implements the Framework

What you will learn

  • How the three framework elements map to Chart Chooser's two filters (Objective and Data Type).
  • The complete workflow from framework analysis to final chart selection.

Remember the framework? Context, Audience, Data, and Chart Selection. Chart Chooser is built on this exact logic. Let me show you how each element maps to the tool.

Mapping 1: Context + Audience = Objective

In the framework, you analyze your context (what problem you're solving) and your audience (who needs the information). These two elements tell you your objective: what you're trying to communicate.

Chart Chooser takes this and makes it practical with the Objectives filter.

Instead of analyzing context and audience from scratch every time, Chart Chooser gives you a list of common objectives:

  • Compare
  • Show trend
  • Show composition
  • Show distribution
  • Show relationship

How to use it: Think about your context and audience, then select the objective that matches.

Example: Context: Board meeting to allocate ressources to the most profitable product, this is a strategic decision. Audience: Executives who need quick insights, in this case the objective is to compare (they need to compare product performance). You select "Compare" in Chart Chooser.

Mapping 2: Data = Data Type

In the framework, you identify your data structure: categorical or continuous

Chart Chooser takes this and makes it practical with the Data Type filter.

You look at your data and select:

  • Categorical (distinct groups)
  • Continuous (measurements on a scale, especially time)

Example: Data: Revenue numbers by product, keep in mind that products are categorical. You select "Categorical" in Chart Chooser.

Mapping 3: Objective + Data Type = Chart Suggestions

In the framework, you combine your objective with your data possibilities to select a chart. Chart Chooser does this automatically. Once you have selected your objective and data type, it shows you appropriate chart options.

For "Compare" + "Categorical," Chart Chooser suggests: Column chart, Bar chart, Radar chart, etc

Each suggestion includes a preview, when to use it, and strengths/limitations.

The Complete Workflow

Step 1: Analyze your situation using the framework

  • Context: What problem am I solving?
  • Audience: Who needs this?
  • Data: What type of data do I have?

Step 2: Translate to Chart Chooser inputs

  • Context + Audience = Select Objective
  • Select Data Type

Step 3: Review suggestions

  • Chart Chooser shows some options
  • Read the descriptions
  • Consider your specific needs

Step 4: Make your selection

  • Choose the chart that best fits your situation
  • Apply design principles (I will cover this in Module 4)

Module 4: Design Basics

Lesson 1: Cognitive Load Basics

What you will learn

  • Why cognitive load matters for your audience's mental capacity.
  • Three quick techniques to reduce cognitive load: order data, use direct labels, and highlight key information.

You have selected the right chart type using the framework and Chart Chooser. But your job isn't done yet. Even the right chart can fail if it is poorly designed. This is where cognitive load comes in.

What is Cognitive Load?

Cognitive load is the mental effort required to process information. Your audience's brain has limited capacity

When someone looks at your chart, their brain is working by reading labels, identifying patterns, comparing values, connecting information, and drawing conclusions. Every element in your chart demands mental effort.

The Principle

Your audience has a fixed amount of mental capacity. Don't waste it on bad design.

Three Quick Wins for Reducing Cognitive Load

1. Order your data

When you arrange columns from highest to lowest, the pattern becomes instantly obvious. The audience doesn't have to work to understand the ranking. Order to show rankings or comparisons, such as Time series, to keep chronological order. Natural categories (S, M, L, XL) to maintain natural order.

2. Use direct labels instead of legends

Instead of forcing eyes to jump between chart and legend, place labels directly on the data points. This eliminates split attention where the brain has to work harder to connect information.

3. Highlight what matters

If Product D is the top seller, make its line a bold color and turn the other lines into light grey. Product D immediately stands out. The audience doesn't have to work to find the key product.

The Design Principle

Every element in your chart should help understanding. If it is not helping, it is hurting.

Ask yourself:

  • Does this color encode information, or is it just decoration?
  • Does this label help or clutter?
  • Does this gridline guide the eye or create noise?
  • Does this order reveal patterns or hide them?

Lesson 2: Accessibility Basics

What you will learn

  • Why your audience needs accessible charts.
  • Visual accessibility techniques: patterns, color, contrast, etc.

Accessibility isn't just about compliance or ticking boxes. It's about ensuring that everyone can use your charts, whether they have a temporary or permanent loss of vision, cognitive ability, or are limited by the technology they are using.

Here's the reality: 15-20% of your audience has some form of visual impairment. That could be colorblindness, low vision, or complete blindness using a screen reader.

If your charts aren't accessible, you're excluding a significant portion of your audience.

What Makes a Chart Accessible?

1. Color is not the only way to distinguish data

This chart uses color to distinguish 3 products. But for someone with red or green colorblindness, some lines look identical.

The fix: Use patterns or shapes in addition to color

Now each line has both a unique color and a unique pattern (dashed, dotted, solid). Even if someone can't see the colors, they can distinguish the lines.

2. Text contrast is sufficient

Labels and titles need sufficient contrast to be readable. The standard: WCAG AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text.

3. Screen readers can navigate the chart

People using screen readers can't see your chart at all. They need an alternative way to understand the data.

Highcharts has an accessibility module that makes charts keyboard-navigable and screen-reader friendly. When enabled, screen reader users can navigate through data points with arrow keys, hear data values announced, and understand the structure and purpose of the chart.

4. Meaningful chart descriptions

Don't just describe what someone can see, provide the insight.

Here I have a bad and a good accessibility description:
Bad description: "A column chart with four bars of different heights."
Good description: "Annual revenue by product. Product D leads with $890K (43% of total), followed by Product A at $650K. Product B shows the lowest performance at $480K."

Quick Accessibility Checklist

  • Use color + pattern/shape to distinguish data
  • Ensure text has sufficient contrast (4.5:1 minimum)
  • Include Highcharts accessibility module
  • Provide meaningful chart descriptions
  • Test keyboard navigation

Course Wrap-Up

You made it! Congratulations on completing the Introduction to Data Visualization Framework Course

You now have a practical framework for creating charts that actually help people make decisions. No more guessing which chart to use or defaulting to whatever feels familiar.

Throughout the course, you have learned how to:

  • Use the three-question framework (Context, Audience, Data) to guide every chart you create.
  • Recognize when you are solving a strategic problem versus an operational one, and adjust your approach accordingly.
  • Make charts that work for your specific audience, whether that is a time-pressed executive or a detail-hungry analyst.
  • Know the difference between categorical and continuous data, and why it matters for chart selection.
  • Turn your framework analysis into actual chart choices using Chart Chooser.
  • Reduce cognitive load, so your audience spends less mental energy decoding and more time understanding.
  • Build accessible charts that work for everyone, including people with visual impairments.

One more thing This framework isn't a rulebook. It is a thinking tool. The goal isn't to mechanically follow steps, it's to ask better questions before you start designing. The more you use it, the more natural it becomes.
